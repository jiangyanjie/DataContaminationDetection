He considered the chambers and his own small temporary residences, urging me to look out at my pockets, and a little room near his door in which he could "behold the shake-down'. When he had made an appointment to his breakfast, and was wiping his knife on his leg, I said this without a particular preface: "After you were down all night, I told my friend of the struggle that the soldiers found you engaged in on the battlefields when we came up. You remember?" "Remember!" said he. "I think so!" "We want to know something about that man - and about you. It's strange to know no more about that, particularly you, I was able to tell last night. Is not one good reason to be such an expert as another for our knowing more?" "Well!" he said, after consideration. "You're in no oath, you know!" "Assuredly," he said. "As to anything I say, you know," he insisted. "The oath applies to all." "I understand you are good - I do so." "And look'ee here! Wotever ! Nothing is worked out for," he insisted again. "So this is the question in my mind." He took out his black shirt and said he was going to stand with negrohead, when, looking at the tangle of tobacco in his hand, he seemed to think it might perplex the rest of his narrative. He took his pants back again, with the pipe in front of his arms. He looked over all the smoke on each knee, and, after turning an angry eye on the fire for some moments, looked round at us and said what follows. Chapter 42 "Dear boy and Pip's ." I am not a-going fur to tell you my life, my own song , my story-book. But to give it you short and handy, I wrote it at a bar, describing a mouthful of words inside and out of jail, in jail and out of jail, in jail and out of jail, in jail and out of jail, in jail and out of prison, in jail and out of prison. In the end you got me back into my life pretty much, down the top of my house. As I was transported, and a few times as I got shipped off, arter Pip stood my way. Mine had been paid for, but still had to, pretty much, get out except hanged. I've thrown my house up, as much as a silver tea-kettle. I had carted here and carted , I put out of this town and put into that town, held in the stocks, and whipped and worried and drove. I have no more , I have no more. I will do and never have - if so much. I first become aware of myself, down in Essex, a thieving turnips for my living.
