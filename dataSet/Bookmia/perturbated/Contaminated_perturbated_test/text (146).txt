And on the trunk of the tree he leaned against, a brown squirrel was also watching him, and from the bush nearby a cock pheasant was delicately stretching his neck to peep out, and even near him were two rabbits sitting up and on their tremulous noses--and actually speaking to the squirrel as if they had never heard from him. Mary tried to watch him and listen to the strange low little call he seemed to hear. When he saw Mary he held his hand down to her that whisper -- almost as low as and rather like his piping. "Don't tha' move," she said. "It'd be tough," she said. Mary remained motionless. He stopped playing against him and began to rise from the ground. He moved so slowly that it seemed as though he had never seen his moving at that time at all. When his hand was on his back then he scampered back up into the branches of his tree, the pheasant withdrew from the end of his tail and was still on all his feet. She would eventually hop away, though not at all as if he'd been frightened. "I'm Dickon," the boy said. "That's Mistress Mary."
