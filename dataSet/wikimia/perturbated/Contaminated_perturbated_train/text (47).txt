S.A.S. v. France, a case brought after an opinion by the European Court of Human Rights which ruled that the French ban on face covering does not violate European Convention on Human Rights's (ECHR) provisions, particularly the right to privacy or to religion, nor other articles of the ECHR. On these two points, the Court held her decision by fifteen votes to two. The two justices in the minority expressed their partly dissenting opinion. The French ban against face covering, loi nÂ° 2010-1192 du 11 octobre 2010 interdisant la dissimulation du visage dans l'espace public, was passed by parliament on 11 October 2010 and entered into force on 25 April 2013. Although the law bans most forms of face covering, it was generally designed to stop face covering and it aimed at banning the niqab.On the same day the law entered into force, a French woman born out of Sudan referred to as S.A.S. brought a civil case against the French state as the law prevented to her from wearing the niqab in public places.The woman argued that the law violated articles 3 (against inhuman or degrading treatment ), 8 (on the right to privacy), 9 (on the freedom of religion), 10 (on the right to freedom of expression), 11 (on the right to freedom of assembly), and 15 (which prohibits discrimination).On the other side, the French state argued with regard to articles 8 and 9, that the exceptions in paragraph 2 of these articles applied.
