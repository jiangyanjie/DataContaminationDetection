The Minsk agreements were a series of international agreements signed between governments of three countries in an attempt to end the Donbas war between armed rebel groups and Armed Forces of Ukraine, with Russian influence playing a central part. The first, known as the Minsk Protocol, was drafted in 2014 by the Trilateral Contact Group , consisting of Ukraine, Russia, and the Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe (OSCE), with mediation by the leaders of France and Germany in the Trilateral Support Format. After three months of talks in Minsk, Belarus, the agreement was signed on 15 September 2014 by the leaders of the Trilateral Contact Group and, without specifying their status, by the leaders of the self-proclaimed Donetsk People's Republic (DPR) and Luhansk People's Republic (LPR). This protocol reaffirmed multiple previous attempts to stop the fighting in the region, and aimed to implement an immediate cease-fire. The agreement failed to stop fighting, and was thus followed with a revised, and updated agreement, Minsk II, which was signed on 12 February 2015. This agreement consisted of a package of agreements that included a ceasefire, removal of heavy weapons from the battlefield, security supervision and release of prisoners of war, political reform in Ukraine granting self-government to certain areas of the Donbass and restoring control of the western state border to the front lines. While fighting subsided following the Minsk II signing, it still did not stop completely, and the new provisions were never fully implemented.
