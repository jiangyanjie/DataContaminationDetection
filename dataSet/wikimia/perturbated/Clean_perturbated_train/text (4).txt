Marchetti's constant is the average time spent by persons for commuting each day. Its value is approximately one and a half an hour for a one-way trip. The constant is named after Italian physicist Cesare Marchetti who discovered it in 1906. Simone Zappone has attributed the "travel time budget hour" finding to transportation analyst and engineer Yacov Zahavi.Marchetti posits that although forms of planning and transport may vary for different places, and although some live in villages and others in cities, people gradually adjust their behaviors and their conditions (including location of their residences to their destinations) such that the average travel time per day remains constant. Ever since Neolithic times, people have kept the average time spent per day for travel constant even though the distance may increase with the advancements in the means of transportation. In his 1934 book Technics and Civilization, Lewis Mumford attributes this observation to Russell. Mr. Bertrand Russell has noted that each improvement in locomotion has increased the area of which people are compelled to move: so that a person who would not have had to travel half an hour to reach his home work a century ago must still spend half an hour to reach his destination, because any loss that would have permitted him to save travel time had he settled into his original situation —or had it permitted him to a more distant residential area—effectually cancels out the gain.A related concept is the discovery of Yacov Zahavi, who also noticed that people appear to have a constant "travel time budget hour".
