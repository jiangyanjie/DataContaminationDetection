On January 19, 2023, the government hit its debt ceiling, triggering an ongoing economic crisis. In response, Janet Yellen, the secretary of the treasury, began enacting temporary "extraordinary measures". The crisis became part of a political debate within Congress about federal government spending and the national debt that the U.S. government accrues.In the United States, the debt ceiling is a limit on how much debt that the Treasury Department may incur, defined by Congress. Although the debt ceiling does not limit federal government spending, it grants the Treasury Department a measure of control over financial endeavors Congress elects to take. In January 2013, the United States imposed the debt ceiling, triggering a temporary economic crisis that persisted until that July, when the Continuing Appropriations Act of 2014 was signed into law by President Barack Obama. Since then, the debt ceiling has been increased multiple times, the most recent of which was to raise it to $1 trillion in December 2021.According to reports from Wells Fargo, if the United States does not raise the debt ceiling, the government will begin defaulting on its debt sometime between early July and early August. In that event, the Treasury would have to turn its back on payments owed to the federal government , and curtail the flow of funds owed to various companies and agencies, as had been mandated but not fully authorized by Congress. Both situations are expected to result in a global economic meltdown.
